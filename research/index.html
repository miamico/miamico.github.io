I explore ways to pull trustworthy insights from noisy superconducting processors. My research ranges from algorithm design and error-aware control to hardware benchmarking, all rooted in non-stationary cavity quantum electrodynamics.

Quantum algorithms

I design and run algorithms that stay within the coherence budget of today’s hardware yet extract key observables. Highlights include diagonalising 50-site spin lattices with Krylov subspaces, measuring central charge on a universal processor, and showing that qubit-level matched filtering can recover astrophysical signals at classical signal-to-noise ratios. The common theme is simple quantum circuits paired with classical post-processing to reach classically intractable regimes sooner.

Error suppression and mitigation

To make algorithms reliable, I develop hardware-aware approaches that suppress and mitigate errors. This includes autonomous pulse calibration using deep reinforcement learning and black-box optimization, compiler methods that cut crosstalk with dynamical decoupling, and qubit-selection schemes built on sparse noise tomography. Recently I’ve pushed these ideas into the algorithms themselves, using tensor-network techniques like multiproduct formulas to boost performance without deeper circuits. The goal across the board is to reduce dominant errors in situ so mitigation overhead stays manageable.

Non-stationary cavity QED

Many superconducting devices rely on tunable qubit–cavity coupling. In my doctoral work I studied fast driving regimes where non-adiabatic phenomena—the dynamical Lamb and Casimir effects—emerge. Circuit models show these effects can generate entanglement and enable ultra-fast gates.
